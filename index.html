What do I mean by scale?

  • Traffic and uptime expectations
  • Large volumes of data and users
  • Many teams and many services
  • Ownership and coordination boundaries
  • Security, regulatory, and compliance pressure
  • Systems that must keep evolving for years

We love software design

and that is why we tend to think this is the most important part of software.

The uncomfortable truth:

at scale, the most expensive mistakes live in architecture, infrastructure, and data.

Every code and architectural decision expires.

The real question is not only what works now, but how it will age.

The hard part is not seeing that something is bad. The hard part is changing it without stopping the world.

Time is part of the design.

  • Schema evolution
  • Deprecation windows
  • Backfills and reprocessing
  • Idempotency and retries
  • Dual writes and compatibility periods
  • Sunsetting old paths

Socio-technical design matters.

  • Team boundaries shape architecture
  • Ownership determines how fast change can happen
  • Decision-making processes can make changes fast or slow
  • Conway's Law is a daily constraint
  • Many migrations fail organizationally before they fail technically

Let's talk code now

Good large-scale code design is not static elegance; it is changeability under load, new information, and business pressure.

If changeability is the goal,

what should code optimize for?

OO is great. Really?

Let’s get concrete:

a more practical design philosophy

Optimize for reversibility, not timelessness.

Get comfortable with half-life designs.

Make failure visible.

If something will hurt, let it hurt in a way you can detect, localize, and roll back.

Treat data and infrastructure as first-class.

They outlive code fashion. They are where migration cost concentrates.

Take the simplest path and do not overengineer.

Predictions age quickly, so do not pay abstraction tax too early.

Be ready to refactor.

Learn from the past, plan the future, and repeat.

What’s in it for you, researchers?

  1. Study software as a changing system, not a static artifact
  2. Include data, infrastructure, operations, and teams in the picture
  3. Focus on decisions, trade-offs, and how they age over time
  4. Help make change safer, cheaper, and more observable
  5. Look where the pain is, not only where measurement is easy

Bring research closer to production reality.

We need more field work, more operational context, and more studies of systems that cannot stop.

A summary

  • Scale means traffic, data, uptime, teams, services, and long-lived change.
  • The hardest design problems are often architectural, infrastructural, data-heavy, and organizational.
  • The only certainty is that today's decisions will age, and refactoring will eventually be necessary.
  • Time, rollout, ownership, and compatibility are part of the design.
  • Code should stay simple, disciplined, and shaped by real pressure.
  • The goal is not timelessness, but reversible and survivable systems.
