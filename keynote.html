Designing for expiration, not certainty.
A belief many of us share
Premise

We love software design

and that is why we tend to think this is the most important part of software.

The premise is familiar.
The rest of the talk complicates it.
Core thesis
Trade-offs expire

Every design decision expires.

The real question is not “What is the right architecture?” but “What pain am I choosing today, and how bad will it be when the context changes?”

Trade-offs age. Assumptions expire.
Regret is part of the job.
  • In large systems, everything is a trade-off.
  • No design gives only benefits; every design also buys you a particular kind of pain.
  • The hardest part is that you do not have a crystal ball. You are deciding under uncertainty.
  • So the real question is not “What is the right architecture?” but “What pain am I choosing today, and how bad will it be when the context changes?”
  • Architectural decisions that once looked reasonable later became liabilities.
  • Regret is not a sign of incompetence.
  • Regret is the normal outcome of designing in a changing environment.
What hurts most at scale
Infrastructure and architecture

The uncomfortable truth:

at scale, the real killers are architecture, infrastructure, and data. Not code.

The biggest pain is usually not the ugly method.
It is the immovable system boundary.
  • Engineers love talking about code quality because code is visible and local.
  • But in very large systems, ugly code is often not what hurts most.
  • What really hurts is infrastructure and architecture that become hard to move.
  • Changing a cache technology.
  • Repartitioning a table.
  • Evolving storage layout.
  • Changing queue semantics.
  • Replacing a foundational dependency.
  • Migrating stateful systems without downtime.
One more twist
Agentic world

In an agentic world, code is even less of a problem.

Local messes get cheaper to read, patch, and reshape.

AI changes local code economics more than global migration economics.
Faster patching is not cheaper rewiring.

AI lowers the cost of reading and patching ugly local code, but it does not lower the cost of migrating the wrong infrastructure or untangling the wrong architecture.

Reframing the pain
Survivable vs. freezing

Bad code is unpleasant, but survivable.

Local messes can be cleaned slowly and independently. Global constraints can freeze whole organizations.

This is not “code quality does not matter.”
It is “code quality is often not the first thing that kills you.”
  • At scale, code quality is often not the dominant constraint. Architecture, state, and infrastructure are usually harder to change.
  • Bad infrastructure or hard architectural boundaries can freeze whole organizations.
  • Local messes are often manageable. Global constraints are what trap you.
  • Not “code quality does not matter,” but rather “at scale, code quality is often not the first thing that kills you.”
Large-scale evolution
Large refactoring at scale

Refactoring at scale

Architectural refactoring is not cleanup; it is the main mechanism by which large systems evolve.

The hard part is not seeing that something is bad. The hard part is changing it without stopping the world.

Migration beats cleanup as the dominant lens.
How do we change safely while production keeps moving?
  • We should care less about static judgments like “this code smells” and more about dynamic questions like: how do I change this safely? how do I migrate incrementally? how do I keep the system working while replacing its internals?
Techniques that make change survivable
Refactor safely

Refactor safely.

  1. Feature flags
  2. Shadow traffic
  3. Dual reads and writes
  4. Rolling upgrades
  5. Backward-compatible APIs
  6. Data migration plans

These are not operational details. They are design tools.

Good design includes the mechanism for being replaced.
Reversibility needs machinery, not just taste.
Definition of good design
Changeability under pressure

Good large-scale design is not static elegance;

it is changeability under load, new information, and business pressure.

The system is good if it can keep moving while reality changes around it.
Mobility is a first-class design outcome.
One sacred cow
OO is great. Really?

OO is great. Really?

OO is powerful when you truly need substitution, rich behavior, extension points, and domain flexibility.

But a lot of business software is mostly orchestrating work, moving data, calling services, handling errors, managing concurrency, and enforcing workflow.

In that world, procedural code is often more direct, cheaper, and more honest.

Sometimes abstraction buys clarity.
Sometimes it buys indirection before value.
  • I'm biased. Two books on OO, one in Portuguese, one in English.
  • Many of us were trained to think that good business software naturally gravitates toward OO-heavy design.
  • OO is powerful when you truly need flexibility, substitution, rich domain behavior, and extension points.
  • But the truth is it is about: orchestrating work, moving data, calling services, handling errors, managing concurrency, enforcing workflow.
  • In that world, procedural code is often more direct, cheaper, and more honest. OO can add abstraction, indirection, and boilerplate before it buys real value.
  • In microservice-heavy systems, explicit error handling and concurrency often matter more than elegant polymorphism.

Time to get concrete
Practical philosophy

Let’s get concrete:

a more practical design philosophy

From critique to operating principles.
Principles for systems that will need to move.
Four orienting statements
Manifesto
1Since design decisions age, the goal is not permanence.
2Since trade-offs are unavoidable, the goal is not purity.
3Since systems change, the goal is not elegance alone.
4The real goal is to make future change less catastrophic.
This is the backbone for the rest of the talk.
Reversibility over righteousness.
Principle 1
Reversibility

Optimize for reversibility, not timelessness.

Get comfortable with half-live designs.

Many good designs are intentionally temporary.
A bridge can be better than a monument.
Principle 2
Visible failure modes

Prefer designs whose failure modes are visible.

If something will hurt, let it hurt in a way you can detect, localize, and roll back.

Opacity turns incidents into archaeology.
Observable pain is manageable pain.
Principle 3
Data and infrastructure boundaries

Treat data and infrastructure boundaries as first-class.

They outlive code fashion. They are where migration cost concentrates.

Most real lock-in lives at boundaries.
Data shape is architecture.
Principle 4
Abstractions with evidence

Choose abstractions only when you have evidence you need them.

Premature flexibility often turns into permanent indirection.

Wait for the shape of variation to become real.
Evidence beats aesthetic symmetry.
Principle 5
Optimization ages

Accept that today’s optimization may become tomorrow’s bottleneck.

The shortcut that saves you now can later trap growth, load patterns, or organization structure.

Success changes the shape of the system.
Scale turns strengths into liabilities.
Principle 6
Migration path

Design with the migration path in mind, not just the steady state.

The replacement strategy is part of the design.

If you cannot explain how to evolve it, you do not fully understand it.
Steady state is only one phase of the system.
Clarification
Against certainty

This is not a talk against architecture.

It is a talk against architectural certainty.

Managing uncertainty beats pretending it is gone.
Leave systems that can still be moved.
  • Large-scale design is an exercise in managing uncertainty, not defeating it.
  • The best engineers are not the ones who always pick the perfect design.
  • They are the ones who understand the trade-offs, recognize when assumptions have expired, and leave systems that can still be moved.
What is in it for research
Research agenda

What’s in it for you, researchers?

  1. Forget code smells :-)
  2. Architectural reversibility
  3. Infrastructure migration patterns
  4. Large-scale refactoring strategies
  5. How decisions age as organizations and traffic evolve

There is a rich research agenda in the systems that cannot stop moving.

Shift attention from judging artifacts to studying evolvability.
There is plenty left to discover beyond local code quality.
Friendly provocation
Fieldwork

Get away from the coziness of your research lab :)

Talk to the teams living inside production constraints.
The most interesting questions are often in the operational mess.
Summary
Closing

A summary

  • Every design decision is a trade-off under uncertainty.
  • At scale, architecture and infrastructure hurt more than code ugliness.
  • Refactoring and migration are the core large-scale design skills.
  • Our abstractions should match the real shape of the work, not our ideology.
  • The goal is not perfect design, but reversible and survivable design.

Design for the day your assumptions stop being true.

Thank the audience and open the floor.
End on reversibility, not perfection.
